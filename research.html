Youth Mental Health Research

Evidence-based insights on adolescent mental wellness

Social Media & Adolescent Mental Health

Large-scale studies (e.g., Orben & Przybylski, 2022) show that the relationship between social media use and mental health is modest but meaningful. Effects vary by individual: some youth benefit from connection, while others experience increased anxiety, depression, and body image concerns.

Key findings:

  • Passive consumption (scrolling without interaction) correlates more strongly with lower well-being than active use.
  • Comparison and FOMO are significant mediators—teens who frequently compare themselves to others online report higher depressive symptoms.
  • Sleep displacement (using screens late at night) amplifies negative effects on mood and cognitive function.
  • Algorithmic feeds that prioritize engagement can expose youth to harmful content and reinforce negative thought patterns.

Sources: Orben & Przybylski (2022), Nature; Haidt (2024), The Anxious Generation

Causes of Depression & Anxiety in Youth

Research indicates a biopsychosocial model: genetics, brain development, life stress, and environment interact.

  • Developmental: Adolescence involves significant brain restructuring; the amygdala (emotion) matures before the prefrontal cortex (regulation), increasing vulnerability to emotional dysregulation.
  • Environmental: Academic pressure, social conflict, family stress, and adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) are robust predictors.
  • Digital environment: Constant connectivity, cyberbullying, and curated perfection can fuel rumination and social anxiety.
  • Protective factors: Strong relationships, sleep, physical activity, and a sense of purpose buffer against depression and anxiety.

Sources: WHO; Kessler et al.; Canadian Mental Health Association

School & Parent Intervention Strategies

Evidence supports multi-tiered, whole-community approaches.

  • Universal prevention: Mental health literacy programs, stress management curricula, and anti-stigma campaigns improve help-seeking and early identification.
  • Targeted support: School-based CBT and mindfulness programs show effectiveness for mild-to-moderate anxiety and depression.
  • Parent involvement: Parent training in emotion coaching, validation, and non-punitive communication reduces conflict and improves youth outcomes.
  • Integration: Models like Foundry BC integrate mental health, primary care, and substance use services—reducing barriers for youth who need multiple supports.

Sources: Werner-Seidler et al.; School Mental Health Ontario; Foundry BC evaluation reports

Prevention Programs: What Works

Systematic reviews identify several evidence-based prevention approaches:

  • Cognitive-Behavioural (CBT) prevention: Teaches youth to identify and challenge unhelpful thoughts and behaviours; reduces incidence of depression and anxiety in at-risk populations.
  • Mindfulness-based programs: Improve emotional regulation and reduce stress; effects are stronger when practiced consistently.
  • Resilience programs: Build coping skills, social support, and problem-solving; most effective when delivered early and reinforced over time.
  • Digital interventions: Apps and online programs can reach youth who avoid in-person services, but should complement—not replace—professional care.

Sources: Calear & Christensen; Merry et al.; Cuijpers et al.
