Psychological Self-Help Manual

Practical tools for managing stress, anxiety, and low mood

1. Grounding Techniques

When anxiety or dissociation feels overwhelming, grounding brings you back to the present.

5-4-3-2-1

1 Name 5 things you can see. 2 Name 4 you can hear. 3 Name 3 you can touch. 4 Name 2 you can smell. 5 Name 1 you can taste.

Cold water

Splash cold water on your face or hold ice. The sensation interrupts spiralling thoughts.

Tip: Practice when calm so it's easier to use in distress.

Source: Dr. Ellen Hendriksen, Center for Anxiety Disorders at Boston University.

2. Breathing Exercises

Slow, controlled breathing activates the parasympathetic nervous system and reduces fight-or-flight.

Box breathing (4-4-4-4)

Breathe in for 4 counts, hold 4, breathe out 4, hold 4. Repeat 4–6 times.

Extended exhale (4-6)

Breathe in for 4 counts, breathe out for 6. Longer exhales calm the body more.

Tip: Place one hand on your belly; let it rise on the inhale and fall on the exhale.

Source: Based on diaphragmatic breathing principles (NIH / Medical News Today).

3. CBT Thought-Challenging

Cognitive restructuring helps you question unhelpful thoughts instead of accepting them as facts.

Steps

  1. Notice the thought (e.g., "No one likes me")
  2. Identify the emotion it triggers
  3. Ask: What's the evidence for and against this thought?
  4. Reframe: What would I tell a friend? What's a more balanced perspective?

Example: "I failed that test" → Evidence against: past successes, one test doesn't define me. Reframe: "I had a hard test; I can learn from this and improve."

Tip: Write your thought, evidence, and new balanced thought in a journal once a day—it trains your brain to do this more automatically.

Source: Evidence-based Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) practices (APA / NIH).

4. Behavioural Activation

Depression often leads to withdrawal. Doing small, enjoyable or meaningful activities can lift mood.

  • Schedule one small activity daily (e.g., 10-minute walk, call a friend)
  • Break tasks into tiny steps—"get dressed" before "go outside"
  • Track what you did and how you felt (1–10) to see patterns
Tip: Don't wait to "feel like it." Action often precedes motivation.

Source: Evidence-based Behavioural Activation protocols for depression (NICE Guidelines).

5. Healthier Social Media Use

  • Set boundaries: Time limits, no phone in bedroom, phone-free meals
  • Curate: Mute or unfollow accounts that make you feel worse
  • Check in: "How do I feel after 10 minutes?" If worse, stop
  • Replace: Use saved time for hobbies, movement, or rest
Tip: Try a 7‑day experiment where you only keep accounts that leave you feeling neutral or better—notice what changes in your mood.

Source: American Psychological Association (APA) and American Academy of Pediatrics digital wellness guidelines.

6. Sleep & Routine

Sleep deeply affects mood and anxiety. Aim for consistent sleep and wake times.

  • No screens 1 hour before bed
  • Keep the bedroom cool and dark
  • If you can't sleep after 20 min, get up, do something calm, then try again
  • Morning light and gentle movement help regulate circadian rhythm
Tip: Protect a “wind‑down ritual” of 15–30 minutes every night (same playlist, same actions) so your brain learns that this routine means sleep is coming.

Source: Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) & Sleep Foundation.

7. When to Seek Professional Help

Self-help supports wellbeing, but it's not a substitute for professional care when needed.

Consider reaching out if:

  • Low mood or anxiety lasts most days for 2+ weeks
  • It's affecting school, relationships, or daily functioning
  • You have thoughts of harming yourself or others
  • Self-help strategies aren't helping
Tip: Before your first appointment, jot down a few bullet points about symptoms, sleep, and recent stresses—this makes it easier to explain what you’re going through.

Source: World Health Organization (WHO) and Crisis Services Canada.
